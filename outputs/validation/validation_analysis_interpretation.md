# Validation Strategy

The validation phase was designed to evaluate the robustness, interpretability, and practical behavior of the proposed pharmacy location prioritization framework. Four complementary analyses were performed.

## 1. Sensitivity Analysis (Script 31) — Highest Importance

This analysis evaluated the robustness of the prioritization framework against systematic changes in component weights. Each criterion weight was increased and decreased while all other parameters remained unchanged. Similarity with the baseline solution was quantified using the Jaccard Index.

Purpose:

- Demonstrate robustness of the framework.
- Identify the most influential weighting parameters.
- Verify that small parameter changes do not substantially alter the recommended locations.

---

## 2. Component Contribution Analysis (Script 32)

This analysis decomposed the final score into its individual components for every selected location.

Purpose:

- Explain why locations receive high scores.
- Identify the dominant decision driver for each selected candidate.
- Improve transparency and interpretability of the framework.

---

## 3. Competition Ablation Experiment (Script 34)

This experiment removed the competition penalty entirely while keeping every other component unchanged.

Purpose:

- Quantify the practical importance of explicitly modelling competition.
- Demonstrate how ignoring competition changes the spatial distribution of recommended locations.
- Compare the resulting locations with the existing pharmacy network.

---

## 4. Variance Decomposition (Script 33) — Supplementary Analysis

This regression-based analysis quantified how much variation in the final score could be attributed to each scoring component.

Purpose:

- Provide an additional statistical explanation of score construction.
- Confirm that the observed component importance is consistent with the mathematical scoring model.

Because the final score is explicitly constructed as a weighted linear combination of the components, this analysis is considered supplementary rather than essential for understanding the proposed framework.


---


**The following sections provide a detailed interpretation of the outputs generated by each validation script, presented sequentially according to their script numbers (Scripts 30–34). Each section explains the objective of the analysis, summarizes the main findings, and discusses their implications for evaluating the robustness and practical applicability of the proposed framework.**

# Script 30 – Spatial Diagnostics

## Objective

This analysis provides a diagnostic evaluation of the spatial characteristics of the final candidate locations selected by the Pharmacy Location Intelligence (PLI) model.

Unlike the optimization procedure itself, these diagnostics do not modify the solution. Instead, they describe the statistical and spatial properties of the selected locations and examine relationships among the normalized scoring components.

---

## Overall Characteristics of the Selected Locations

Among the 100 selected candidate locations:

| Metric | Value |
|--------|------:|
| Total candidates | 100 |
| Mean final score | 63.47 |
| Median final score | 58.42 |
| Maximum final score | 94.86 |
| Minimum final score | 48.13 |
| Mean distance to nearest existing pharmacy | 443.98 m |

### Interpretation

The relatively wide range of final scores indicates that the optimization process identifies locations with varying degrees of overall suitability rather than producing a homogeneous set of nearly identical candidates.

The average nearest-pharmacy distance of approximately **444 meters** confirms that the spatial exclusion procedure successfully prevents excessive clustering while still allowing candidate locations to remain sufficiently close to existing service networks where appropriate.

This distance should not be interpreted as an optimization objective itself, but rather as evidence that the minimum-distance constraint effectively shapes the spatial distribution of recommended sites.

---

## Correlation among Scoring Components

Correlation analysis reveals several important relationships.

### Prescription Demand

Prescription demand shows a very strong positive correlation with the final score:

**r = 0.84**

### Interpretation

Prescription demand explains most of the variation in the composite score.

This finding is expected because prescription volume directly reflects healthcare demand and therefore becomes the principal determinant of candidate ranking after spatial constraints have been satisfied.

---

### Population Accessibility

Population accessibility exhibits a modest positive correlation with the final score:

**r = 0.29**

### Interpretation

Population contributes positively to candidate evaluation but explains considerably less variation than prescription demand.

This suggests that many selected locations already satisfy the population criterion, leaving prescription demand to differentiate among otherwise similar candidates.

---

### Road Accessibility

Road accessibility shows only a weak positive relationship with the final score:

**r = 0.09**

### Interpretation

Road accessibility acts primarily as a secondary refinement criterion rather than as a dominant driver of candidate ranking.

Its contribution improves accessibility but rarely changes the overall ordering of highly competitive locations.

---

### Competition

Competition displays virtually no correlation with the final score:

**r ≈ −0.01**

### Interpretation

Although competition is explicitly incorporated into the optimization model, it behaves as a spatial penalty rather than a ranking variable.

The weak correlation indicates that the greedy spatial filtering already removes most highly competitive candidates before final scoring is performed.

Consequently, competition contributes little additional variation among the remaining selected locations.

---

## Relationships among Input Variables

The strongest correlation among predictor variables is observed between prescription demand and competition:

**r = 0.44**

### Interpretation

Areas with high prescription demand naturally tend to attract existing pharmacies.

This relationship reflects real-world market behavior rather than a methodological artifact.

The optimization framework therefore balances two competing objectives:

- prioritizing areas with high healthcare demand,
- avoiding excessive concentration around existing pharmacies.

The relatively moderate magnitude of this correlation indicates that these objectives are related but not redundant.

---

## Overall Interpretation

The spatial diagnostics indicate that the proposed optimization framework exhibits internally consistent behavior.

Prescription demand is the primary determinant of candidate ranking, while population accessibility provides additional support.

Competition mainly functions as a spatial constraint, and road accessibility serves as a practical refinement criterion.

Together, these findings demonstrate that the selected pharmacy locations emerge from a balanced integration of demand, accessibility, and redundancy avoidance rather than from dependence on any single variable.

# Script 31 – Sensitivity Analysis

## Objective

This analysis evaluates the robustness of the Pharmacy Location Intelligence (PLI) model against uncertainty in expert-defined weights.

Each major component weight was independently perturbed by:

- ±5%
- ±10%
- ±20%

while keeping the remaining weights fixed. After each perturbation, the complete site-selection procedure (including spatial filtering) was executed again, and the resulting candidate set was compared with the baseline solution.

Similarity between the baseline and perturbed solutions was measured using the **Jaccard Similarity Index**.

---

## Overall Interpretation

The proposed locations remain highly stable under moderate changes in the weighting scheme.

Most perturbations changed only a small proportion of selected locations, indicating that the optimization is driven primarily by the underlying spatial structure of the data rather than by fine-tuning of expert weights.

This is an important property because decision-support systems intended for policy applications should not produce completely different recommendations after small subjective adjustments in expert preferences.

---

## Component-wise Interpretation

### Population Accessibility

Population accessibility exhibited the highest robustness.

Even after changing its weight by ±20%, the selected candidate set remained highly similar to the baseline (Jaccard ≈ 0.94).

This suggests that population accessibility forms a stable backbone of the optimization process.

Because many high-scoring locations already possess favorable population characteristics, increasing or decreasing its weight has only a limited influence on the final spatial configuration.

---

### Prescription Demand

Prescription demand showed moderate sensitivity.

Increasing its weight gradually shifted the model toward prescription-intensive areas, producing a larger number of substituted locations.

However, even a ±10% perturbation preserved more than 70% of the selected locations.

This indicates that prescription demand is influential but does not dominate the optimization independently.

---

### Competition Penalty

Competition demonstrated asymmetric behavior.

Increasing the competition penalty produced relatively modest changes.

Conversely, reducing the competition penalty caused substantially larger changes in the selected locations.

This result is expected because relaxing the competition constraint allows many candidate sites located near existing pharmacies to become competitive, thereby increasing the number of alternative high-scoring solutions.

The observed asymmetry confirms that competition functions primarily as a spatial exclusion mechanism rather than as a traditional scoring criterion.

---

### Road Accessibility

Road accessibility produced the largest sensitivity.

Increasing its weight shifted recommendations toward major transportation corridors.

Completely removing the road component generated the lowest similarity with the baseline solution (Jaccard ≈ 0.37), indicating that road accessibility substantially influences the spatial distribution of recommended locations.

Nevertheless, even in this extreme scenario, approximately one-third of the recommended sites remained unchanged, demonstrating that the remaining criteria continue to identify many of the same strategic locations.

---

## Practical Implications

The sensitivity analysis demonstrates that the proposed decision-support framework is **robust but not rigid**.

Small modifications in expert-defined weights produce only limited changes in the final recommendations, whereas large and intentionally unrealistic perturbations reveal predictable shifts in spatial priorities.

This behavior is desirable because:

- The model is stable enough for practical decision making.
- The influence of each policy preference remains transparent.
- Decision makers can deliberately adjust priorities without causing unpredictable model behavior.

Overall, the sensitivity analysis supports the reliability and practical applicability of the proposed pharmacy location optimization framework.

# Script 32 – Component Contribution Analysis

## Objective

This analysis investigates which components of the scoring model are primarily responsible for the selection of candidate pharmacy locations.

Rather than evaluating only the final composite score, the analysis decomposes the contribution of each scoring component to understand the relative importance of prescription demand, existing competition, population coverage and road accessibility.

---

# Driver of Selection

Among the 100 selected candidate locations:

| Primary Driver | Count | Percent |
|----------------|------:|--------:|
| Population     | 64    | 64.0% |
| Prescription   | 36    | 36.0% |
| Competition    | 0     | 0.0% |
| Road           | 0     | 0.0% |

## Interpretation

The proposed model is primarily driven by two variables:

- Population coverage
- Prescription demand

Population coverage is the dominant driver for nearly two-thirds of the selected locations.

This indicates that, after enforcing the minimum-distance constraint and applying all penalties, underserved population clusters become the strongest determinant of candidate selection.

Prescription demand remains the dominant factor for approximately one-third of the selected locations, indicating that high service demand continues to influence prioritization even after accounting for competition.

Road accessibility and competition never become the dominant contributors by themselves.

This result is expected because:

- road accessibility acts as a secondary preference rather than a primary objective;
- competition is incorporated as a penalty, reducing scores instead of increasing them.

---

# Mean Component Contributions

Average contribution among selected locations:

| Component | Mean Contribution |
|-----------|-----------------:|
| Population | 28.78 |
| Prescription | 26.30 |
| Road | 11.20 |
| Competition penalty | 2.82 |

## Interpretation

Population and prescription demand contribute comparable magnitudes to the overall score.

Road accessibility provides a moderate positive contribution.

The average competition penalty is relatively small.

This suggests that the majority of selected candidates are already located sufficiently far from existing pharmacies, meaning that the spatial exclusion strategy successfully filters locations before the competition penalty becomes substantial.

---

# Correlation Analysis

Correlation matrix shows:

- Final score is strongly correlated with prescription contribution (r ≈ 0.84).
- Final score has a moderate positive correlation with population contribution (r ≈ 0.29).
- Road contribution exhibits only a weak relationship with final score (r ≈ 0.09).
- Competition penalty is essentially uncorrelated with the final score (r ≈ −0.01).

## Interpretation

Prescription demand explains most of the variation in the final ranking once candidates satisfy the spatial constraints.

Population coverage contributes additional differentiation but with lower variance because many selected locations already receive high population scores.

Competition penalty contributes little variance because most selected candidates have already avoided dense pharmacy clusters through the spatial filtering stage.

Road accessibility behaves as a refinement criterion rather than a ranking determinant.

---

# Distribution of Component Scores

The descriptive statistics indicate:

- Population scores are highly concentrated near their maximum value (mean 28.78 of 30).
- Prescription contributions exhibit much larger variability (SD ≈ 15.64).
- Competition penalties are zero for most selected locations.
- Road contributions are discrete because they depend on road class weighting.

## Interpretation

Population acts as a threshold variable: almost every selected location already serves a large surrounding population.

Prescription demand therefore becomes the principal factor separating high-ranking candidates from lower-ranking candidates.

---

## Discriminative Power of Individual Components

An additional observation emerges from the variability of the component contributions.

Among the selected locations, the population component exhibits very limited variation (mean = 28.78, SD = 1.84, maximum possible = 30). Most selected candidates therefore receive nearly the maximum achievable population score.

In contrast, the prescription component shows substantially larger variation (mean = 26.30, SD = 15.64), indicating that prescription demand is the principal source of differentiation among high-quality candidate locations.

### Interpretation

These findings suggest that the candidate selection process operates in two stages.

First, the model identifies locations that satisfy a minimum population requirement, ensuring that almost all retained candidates serve sufficiently large populations.

Once this threshold has effectively been satisfied, population ceases to be the primary ranking criterion.

Instead, prescription demand becomes the dominant factor that differentiates candidate locations with otherwise similar demographic characteristics.

From a methodological perspective, this behaviour is desirable because it indicates that the optimization process first guarantees social need (population coverage) and subsequently prioritizes operational demand (prescription volume) when allocating new pharmacy locations.

---

# Overall Interpretation of script 32

The contribution analysis demonstrates that the proposed decision-support framework does not rely on a single criterion.

Instead:

1. Population coverage determines where new pharmacies are socially needed.

2. Prescription demand differentiates priority among these underserved locations.

3. Competition acts as a protective constraint preventing redundant clustering.

4. Road accessibility improves practical accessibility but rarely dominates the decision.

Overall, the results suggest that the proposed multi-criteria scoring framework balances equity (population), demand (prescriptions), redundancy avoidance (competition), and accessibility (road network), while preserving interpretable contributions from each component.

# Script 33 — Variance Decomposition Analysis

## Objective

This analysis quantified the relative statistical contribution of each scoring component to the final composite score.

Unlike the previous component contribution analysis (Script 32), which focused on identifying the dominant driver for each selected location, this analysis evaluated the overall explanatory power of each component across all candidate locations using regression-based variance decomposition.

---

## Main Findings

The regression model reproduced the final score with an R² equal to 1.00.

This result was expected because the final score is mathematically constructed from the four scoring components. Therefore, the full-model R² should not be interpreted as evidence of predictive performance but rather as an internal consistency check confirming the correctness of the scoring implementation.

More informative are the relative importance metrics of each component.

### Marginal explanatory power (Marginal R²)

When each component was evaluated individually:

| Component | Marginal R² |
|-----------|------------:|
| Prescription | 0.707 |
| Population | 0.086 |
| Road accessibility | 0.008 |
| Competition | 0.0001 |

Prescription demand alone explained approximately 71% of the variation in the final score, indicating that prescription demand is the dominant determinant of candidate ranking.

Population explained a much smaller proportion of variance, while road accessibility and competition individually explained very little because their influence is primarily realized through the combined scoring equation.

---

### Standardized regression coefficients

The standardized coefficients ranked the relative influence of the four components after controlling for one another.

| Component | Standardized β |
|-----------|---------------:|
| Prescription | +1.10 |
| Competition | −0.49 |
| Road accessibility | +0.32 |
| Population | +0.13 |

Prescription demand exerted the strongest positive influence on the final score.

Competition showed the strongest negative influence, which is consistent with its role as a penalty within the scoring framework.

Road accessibility contributed positively with moderate magnitude, while population had a comparatively smaller positive effect.

---

### Incremental contribution (ΔR²)

Removing each component from the scoring equation produced the following reductions in explained variance:

| Component | ΔR² |
|-----------|-----:|
| Prescription | 0.906 |
| Competition | 0.193 |
| Road accessibility | 0.097 |
| Population | 0.016 |

Prescription demand accounted for the overwhelming majority of the information contained in the composite score.

Competition remained the second most influential factor despite acting as a penalty.

Road accessibility contributed a moderate amount of unique information, whereas population added only a small amount beyond the other components.

---

## Interpretation

This analysis confirms that the proposed scoring framework is primarily driven by prescription demand while still incorporating meaningful adjustments for competition and accessibility.

However, because the final score is explicitly computed from these four components, this analysis should be interpreted as an internal validation of the scoring architecture rather than an external validation of model performance.

For manuscript purposes, the results of Scripts 30–32 provide stronger evidence of model robustness and practical validity, whereas the present analysis mainly demonstrates internal consistency of the composite scoring formulation.

# 34. Competition Ablation Analysis

## Objective

To evaluate the practical importance of the **competition component**, the site-selection model was executed a second time after completely removing the competition penalty (Competition Weight = 0).

The resulting candidate set was then compared with the baseline model.

---

## Results

### 1. Change in the Selected Candidate Set

| Metric | Value |
|--------|------:|
| Jaccard Similarity | **0.351** |

Only **35.1%** of the selected candidate locations remained unchanged after removing the competition component.

In other words, approximately **65%** of the selected locations were replaced when competition was ignored.

This represents the largest structural change observed among all validation analyses conducted in this study.

---

### 2. Distance to the Nearest Existing Pharmacy

| Metric | Value |
|--------|------:|
| Mean distance | **309.6 m** |
| Median distance | **245.0 m** |
| Minimum distance | **13.3 m** |
| Maximum distance | **1204.6 m** |

---

### 3. Overlap with Existing Pharmacies

| Distance to Existing Pharmacy | Percentage of Selected Locations |
|------------------------------|---------------------------------:|
| < 250 m | **51%** |
| < 500 m | **80%** |
| < 750 m | **90%** |
| < 1000 m | **99%** |

---

## Interpretation

The ablation experiment demonstrates that removing the competition component substantially alters the spatial configuration of the recommended pharmacy locations.

Without the competition penalty, the model tends to prioritize areas with high prescription demand and population density, even if these locations are already well served by existing pharmacies.

As a result, the selected candidates become highly concentrated around current pharmacies, with more than half located within only **250 meters** of an existing pharmacy and **80%** located within **500 meters**.

These findings indicate that the competition component functions primarily as a **spatial regularization mechanism**, preventing excessive clustering of newly proposed pharmacies in already saturated areas.

Importantly, the competition component is **not the strongest contributor to the numerical final score**, as shown in the variance decomposition analysis. However, it exerts the **greatest influence on the spatial distribution of selected sites**.

This distinction highlights an important characteristic of the proposed framework: the statistical contribution of a variable to the final score is not necessarily equivalent to its influence on the spatial allocation of facilities. Some variables mainly affect ranking, whereas others govern the geographic structure of the final recommendations.

---

## Conclusion

The competition ablation analysis confirms that the competition component is an essential element of the proposed site-selection framework.

Removing this component results in a dramatically different set of candidate locations, most of which are positioned close to existing pharmacies. Therefore, incorporating competition into the scoring framework is necessary to reduce service overlap and to achieve a more balanced spatial distribution of future pharmacy locations.
